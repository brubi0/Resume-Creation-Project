# Resume Rules — Early Career

For candidates with less than 3 years of professional experience. These rules replace (not supplement) the standard resume_rules.md for early-career candidates.

---

## The 6 Evaluation Criteria (Adjusted)

### 1. Clarity
Same as experienced — logical structure, clear headers, no jargon.

### 2. Focus
- Resume is targeted to a specific entry-level role or field
- Every item supports the candidate's direction
- Irrelevant coursework and activities are cut

### 3. Quantification (Relaxed)
- Numbers are preferred but not mandatory on every bullet
- "What I did and what happened" is acceptable when numbers don't exist
- Scale from coursework counts ("built a database tracking 500+ records")
- Transferable numbers from non-industry jobs count ("served 200+ customers daily")
- **Still push for numbers wherever possible** — estimates are fine

### 4. Results-Driven (Adjusted)
- Show what you produced or contributed, not just what you attended or participated in
- Format: "Did X using Y" or "Did X, resulting in Y"
- Outcomes include: deliverables completed, skills demonstrated, feedback received, problems solved
- "Completed" and "delivered" are acceptable when there's no business metric

### 5. Cuts Through the Noise
- Top third must communicate: who you are, what you studied, what you can do, and your best proof point
- Education and strongest project should be visible immediately

### 6. Skimability
Same as experienced — bold key details, consistent formatting, short bullets.

## Resume Structure (Mandatory Order — Early Career)

The structure is different from experienced candidates. Education leads because it's the strongest credential.

### 1. Header
- Name + Target Title (e.g., "JANE SMITH | Aspiring Financial Analyst" or "JANE SMITH | Junior NetSuite Administrator")
- Using "Aspiring", "Junior", or "Entry-Level" is fine — it sets expectations honestly
- Contact info (phone, email, LinkedIn)
- Portfolio URL or GitHub (if relevant to the field)

### 2. Summary (2-3 lines)
- Lead with education and focus area
- Include 1-2 proof points (best project, certification, relevant experience)
- End with what you're looking for
- Tone: enthusiastic and forward-looking, not "proven track record"

**Example:**
> Recent Accounting graduate from University of Houston with hands-on NetSuite experience through SuiteFoundation certification and a capstone project automating month-end close procedures for a simulated $5M company. Completed 3 relevant certifications while working part-time. Seeking an entry-level NetSuite Administrator role where I can grow into full-cycle ERP management.

### 3. Education
This is the #1 section — not buried at the bottom.

- **Degree, Major, School, Graduation Date** — all on one line
- **GPA** — include if 3.3+ or if commonly required in the field
- **Honors / Dean's List / Scholarships** — list them
- **Relevant Coursework** — 4-6 courses max, formatted as a single line
- **Capstone / Thesis** — if strong, give it its own bullet with description

**Example:**
> **B.S. Accounting** — University of Houston, May 2025 | GPA: 3.6 | Dean's List (4 semesters)
>
> Relevant Coursework: Financial Accounting, Managerial Accounting, Database Management, Business Analytics, ERP Systems
>
> Capstone: Designed automated month-end close workflow for simulated $5M manufacturing company using NetSuite sandbox, reducing close time by 40% vs. manual process

### 4. Projects (If Strong Enough for Own Section)
For candidates whose best proof points come from projects, not jobs:

- Treat each project like a mini role: title, context, what you did, tools used, outcome
- 2-4 bullets per project
- Include class projects, personal projects, hackathons, competitions

### 5. Experience
- **Internships first** (most relevant)
- **Part-time / seasonal jobs second** (transferable skills)
- Extract transferable skills with numbers where possible
- 2-4 bullets per role
- Don't apologize for non-industry jobs — frame what's transferable

### 6. Certifications & Technical Skills
- For early career, list more generously than you would for seniors
- Group by category
- Note proficiency honestly (don't claim Expert on a tool from one class)
- In-progress certifications are worth including — they show initiative

### 7. Activities & Leadership (Optional)
- Include if it demonstrates leadership, initiative, or commitment
- Must have specifics: "President of Accounting Club (45 members)" not just "Member of Accounting Club"
- Cut if the resume is already 1 page without it

## Formatting Rules

- **1 page maximum. No exceptions.** Early-career resumes that go to 2 pages signal poor judgment.
- Bold key details (GPA, numbers, tool names)
- No tables for content
- Standard section headers for ATS
- Start bullets with action verbs
- Keep bullets to 1-2 lines max

### Acceptable Verbs for Early Career
Unlike experienced resumes which ban "assisted" and "helped," these are conditionally OK for early career **if followed by specifics:**
- "Assisted with X by doing Y" — acceptable (shows what you actually did)
- "Assisted with X" — not acceptable (what did you do?)
- "Helped" — same rule. Must say what you specifically contributed.

### Verbs to Prefer
Built, Created, Designed, Developed, Analyzed, Researched, Configured, Implemented, Organized, Led, Coordinated, Completed, Delivered, Presented, Automated

---

## Weak Bullet Filter (Early Career)

| Red Flag | Example | Fix |
|----------|---------|-----|
| Participation without contribution | "Member of Accounting Club" | What did you do in the club? Or cut it |
| Duties without outcome | "Responsible for filing documents" | "Organized and digitized 500+ client files, reducing retrieval time" |
| Vague skill claim | "Familiar with Excel" | "Excel (pivot tables, VLOOKUP, data validation) — used in 4 courses" |
| Listing job duties from a description | "Answered phones and greeted customers" | "First point of contact for 50+ daily customer inquiries, maintaining 95%+ satisfaction score" |
| "Helped" or "Assisted" without specifics | "Helped with data entry" | "Entered and validated 200+ records weekly with <1% error rate" |
| Padding with irrelevant coursework | Listing every class taken | 4-6 relevant courses max |

## The 7-Second Test (Early Career Version)

A recruiter scanning for 7 seconds should immediately see:
1. What you studied and where (education is your credential)
2. What you can do (skills and tools)
3. Your best proof point (strongest project or experience)
4. That you're serious about this field (certifications, relevant activities)

If the resume reads like a generic new grad, it fails.

---

## ATS Rules

Same as experienced candidates:
- No tables for content
- Standard section headers
- Include keywords from job postings
- Simple formatting only

---

## Final Checklist (Early Career)

- [ ] Header includes name + target title (with "Junior", "Aspiring", or "Entry-Level")
- [ ] Summary is 2-3 lines with education, best proof point, and what you're seeking
- [ ] Education is the first content section with degree, GPA (if strong), honors, relevant coursework
- [ ] At least 2-3 projects or experiences have specific deliverables and tools listed
- [ ] Transferable skills extracted from non-industry jobs (with numbers where possible)
- [ ] Skills section lists tools with context (where learned, how used)
- [ ] No padding (generic club memberships, irrelevant coursework, duty-only bullets)
- [ ] Strictly 1 page
- [ ] No ATS-hostile formatting
- [ ] Resume passes the early-career 7-second test
