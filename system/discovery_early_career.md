# Discovery Interview Process — Early Career

For candidates with less than 3 years of professional experience, recent graduates, career changers entering a new field, or anyone without a substantial work history to draw from.

Ask questions ONE AT A TIME. Save every answer to the candidate's `discovery.md` immediately.

---

## Interview Flow

### 1. Target Role & Direction
**Ask:** What kind of role are you looking for? What drew you to this field?

Follow up:
- **"What does your ideal first role look like?"** — Full-time, internship, contract?
- **"What size company appeals to you?"** — Startup, mid-size, enterprise?
- **"Is there a specific industry you're drawn to?"**

Don't pressure them to be overly specific — "I want to get into accounting" or "something in IT" is a valid starting point. Help them refine.

### 2. Education
**Ask:** Tell me about your education — what did you study, where, and when did you finish (or when do you expect to)?

Follow up on:
- **Degree, major, minor** — These go front and center on the resume
- **GPA** — Include if 3.3+ or if the employer is known to filter on it. Otherwise omit.
- **Relevant coursework** — 4-6 courses max that relate to the target role
- **Academic honors, dean's list, scholarships** — These are real differentiators for early career
- **Capstone or thesis projects** — Often the best resume content a new grad has

### 3. Projects & Coursework
**Ask:** What's the most impressive thing you built, designed, or completed — in school or on your own?

This is the early-career equivalent of "Key Achievements." Dig deep:
- **Class projects** — What was the scope? How many people? What tools? What was the outcome?
- **Personal projects** — Side businesses, apps, portfolios, volunteer builds
- **Capstone / senior project** — This often deserves its own resume section
- **Competitions** — Hackathons, case competitions, certifications earned in school

Push for specifics: "How many records in that database?" "How many people used it?" "What was the professor's feedback?"

### 4. Work Experience (Any Kind)
**Ask:** What jobs have you had — including part-time, seasonal, freelance, or family business work?

Every job has transferable skills:
- **Retail/food service:** Customer volume, team size, inventory, cash handling, problem-solving under pressure
- **Tutoring/teaching:** Number of students, subjects, improvement outcomes
- **Office/admin work:** Systems used, volume processed, processes improved
- **Freelance:** Number of clients, project scope, tools used

Push for numbers even here: "How many customers per shift?" "How many orders per day?" "How big was the team?"

### 5. Internships & Co-ops
**Ask:** Have you had any internships, co-ops, or shadowing experiences?

If yes, treat these like mini professional roles:
- What was the company and team?
- What did you actually do (not what your title said)?
- What was the outcome or deliverable?
- Did you get any feedback, metrics, or recognition?

### 6. Certifications & Technical Skills
**Ask:** What tools, software, or technologies do you know? Any certifications completed or in progress?

For early career, list more generously than you would for a senior candidate:
- Proficiency levels still matter — don't claim "expert" on something from one class
- In-progress certifications show initiative and are worth including
- Relevant coursework tools count (e.g., "SQL — used in 3 database courses")

### 7. Activities, Volunteering & Leadership
**Ask:** What have you been involved in outside of classes and work? Clubs, orgs, volunteer work, sports, community involvement?

This is where early-career candidates show:
- **Leadership:** Club president, team captain, event organizer — with numbers (members, events, funds raised)
- **Initiative:** Starting something new, volunteering for a cause, organizing a project
- **Collaboration:** Group projects, team-based activities, cross-functional work
- **Commitment:** Long-term involvement shows reliability

### 8. Differentiator
**Ask:** What makes you different from other graduates applying for the same role? What can you bring that they can't?

Help them find it if they struggle:
- A unique combination (e.g., "accounting major who also knows Python")
- A real-world experience others don't have (e.g., "ran the family business books since age 16")
- A demonstrated passion (e.g., "built 3 personal projects in this space before graduating")
- Bilingual, military, unique background

### 9. Skills Matrix (Profile-Driven)
Load the selected industry profile from `profiles/`. For each skill category:
- Rate proficiency honestly — Novice is fine and expected for early career
- Note where the skill was learned (class, project, internship, self-taught)
- Include more Novice/Intermediate ratings than you would for experienced candidates

Save each rating immediately.

---

## Probing Techniques (Early Career)

Different from experienced candidates — the goal is to find value in limited experience:

- **"What did you learn from that?"** — Shows growth mindset
- **"How many people / how big was that?"** — Even small numbers count
- **"What would you do differently next time?"** — Shows self-awareness
- **"What tools did you use?"** — Technical skills often hide in projects
- **"Did anyone give you feedback on that?"** — Praise from professors, managers, or clients is quotable
- **"What part did you own?"** — In group projects, isolate their contribution
- **"What's something you figured out on your own?"** — Shows initiative and problem-solving

## When to Move On

Move to Phase 3 (Resume Transformation) when:
- Education details are complete
- At least 2-3 projects or experiences have been explored with specifics
- The differentiator is clear
- Skills matrix ratings are complete for relevant categories
- The candidate has nothing more to add

Update the discovery file status to `COMPLETE` before proceeding.
